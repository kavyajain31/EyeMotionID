## 1. Introduction to Behavioral Biometrics

Biometric authentication systems are broadly classified into two categories - **static (physiological) biometrics** and **dynamic (behavioral) biometrics**. Static biometrics such as fingerprint recognition, facial recognition, and iris recognition rely on fixed physical characteristics of a person. While these systems achieve high recognition accuracy, they are vulnerable to spoofing attacks such as fake fingerprints, face masks, and printed iris images.

Behavioral biometrics, on the other hand, capture the unique way a person interacts with the world - such as typing rhythm, gait patterns, signature dynamics, and eye movement behavior. Since behavioral patterns are generated by the neurological and muscular activity of a person, they are significantly harder to replicate or spoof.

Human eye movement behavior is one of the most distinctive and difficult-to-imitate behavioral signals. Eye movements are controlled by a complex combination of neurological processes, cognitive load, and individual motor patterns. Research has shown that blink rhythm, gaze transition patterns, and pupil movement dynamics are consistent within an individual and uniquely different across individuals, making them highly suitable for behavioral biometric authentication.

## 2. Eye Movement Authentication - Key Research Papers

### 2.1 Deep Eyedentification - Jager et al. (2020)

Jager et al. introduced a deep convolutional architecture for biometric identification using involuntary micro-movements of the eye. The system processed raw eye-tracking signals directly without manual feature engineering. The proposed approach achieved a significantly lower error rate compared to traditional feature-based methods, demonstrating that deep learning can effectively capture fine-grained eye movement patterns for user identification.

**Key contribution:** End-to-end deep learning on raw eye-tracking signals for user identification.  
**Relevance to EyeMotionID:** Motivates the use of CNN-based spatial feature extraction on raw eye image sequences.

---

### 2.2 Eye Know You Too - Lohr and Komogortsev (2022)

Lohr and Komogortsev studied both involuntary micro-movements and saccadic macro-movements as biometric signals. The paper demonstrated that even short five-second gaze segments contain enough behavioral information for reliable user authentication. The study compared multiple feature extraction strategies and showed that combining different eye movement types improves identification accuracy.

**Key contribution:** Short gaze segments are sufficient for behavioral biometric authentication.  
**Relevance to EyeMotionID:** Justifies using short temporal sequences of eye frames as input to the LSTM module.

---

### 2.3 RNN/LSTM for Eye Movement Biometrics - Jia et al.

Jia et al. introduced deep learning into eye movement biometrics by building a recurrent neural network using LSTM cells with a softmax classification layer. The system was validated on a dataset of 32 subjects and demonstrated that sequential eye movement patterns can be learned effectively by LSTM networks for user identification.

**Key contribution:** First application of LSTM to eye movement biometric identification.  
**Relevance to EyeMotionID:** Directly motivates the LSTM temporal module in the proposed CNN + LSTM pipeline.

---

### 2.4 Deep Learning for Reading Eye Movement Analysis - ScienceDirect (2020)

This paper proposed a computational model that takes text, fixation, and linguistic feature sequences as inputs to identify users by measuring similarity between predicted and actual fixation sequences. The system achieved 86.5% Rank-1 accuracy on the test set, demonstrating that sequential eye movement analysis can effectively distinguish between users.

**Key contribution:** Fixation sequence modeling for user identification using deep learning.  
**Relevance to EyeMotionID:** Supports temporal sequence modeling approach using gaze fixation data from MPIIGaze dataset.

---

### 2.5 Extensive Study of User Identification via Eye Movements - arXiv (2021)

This study found that combining optimal IVT (Identification Velocity Threshold) parameters, higher-order derivative features, and a blink classifier together can increase identification accuracy by up to 9% depending on the dataset. The study improved BioEye 2015 competition accuracy from 86% to 96% using this combined approach.

**Key contribution:** Blink classification combined with gaze features significantly improves identification accuracy.  
**Relevance to EyeMotionID:** Supports combining blink rhythm analysis with gaze tracking in the feature extraction pipeline.

---

### 2.6 Neural Network-Based Eye Movement Biometrics - Harezlak and Pluciennik (2025)

This paper developed two neural-network-based biometric identification methods using feature vectors constructed from eye movement dynamics including velocity, acceleration, and jerk. LSTM was used as the core temporal model for learning sequential behavioral patterns.

**Key contribution:** Velocity, acceleration, and jerk features from eye movements improve LSTM-based identification.  
**Relevance to EyeMotionID:** Suggests additional temporal features beyond raw pixel values for the LSTM input in Week 3.

## 3. Deep Learning Architectures for Eye Movement Analysis

### 3.1 Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN)

CNNs are widely used for spatial feature extraction from eye images. Architectures such as ResNet18 and MobileNetV2 are commonly applied to extract eye-region features from individual frames. ResNet18 uses residual connections to enable training of deeper networks without vanishing gradients, while MobileNetV2 is a lightweight alternative suitable for faster inference.

**In EyeMotionID:** ResNet18 is used as the CNN backbone to extract 512-dimensional spatial feature vectors from each eye frame. MobileNetV2 is used for lightweight comparative experiments.

### 3.2 Long Short-Term Memory Networks (LSTM)

LSTM networks are a type of recurrent neural network designed to learn sequential dependencies over time. They use gating mechanisms (input gate, forget gate, output gate) to selectively remember and forget information across time steps. This makes them ideal for learning temporal patterns in eye movement sequences such as blink rhythm and gaze transitions.

**In EyeMotionID:** A 2-layer LSTM with hidden size 256 processes the sequence of CNN feature vectors extracted from consecutive eye frames to generate a behavioral biometric signature.

### 3.3 Gated Recurrent Units (GRU)

GRU is a simplified variant of LSTM with fewer parameters. It uses only two gates (reset gate and update gate) instead of three, making it faster to train while maintaining similar performance for sequential modeling tasks.

**In EyeMotionID:** GRU is used as an alternative to LSTM for comparative experiments to identify the better temporal model for this task.

### 3.4 CNN + LSTM Pipeline

The combination of CNN for spatial feature extraction and LSTM for temporal sequence modeling is the core architecture of EyeMotionID. This hybrid approach allows the model to simultaneously capture the appearance of the eye region (spatial) and the behavioral patterns across frames (temporal).

```
Input Frames -> ResNet18 (CNN) -> Feature Vectors -> LSTM -> User Identity
```

## 5. Research Gaps and Project Contribution

### 5.1 Identified Gaps in Existing Work

Existing biometric authentication systems have the following limitations:

- **Static biometrics are vulnerable to spoofing** - fingerprints and iris images can be replicated using physical or digital means.
- **Most eye-based systems focus on static iris texture** - they do not capture the dynamic behavioral patterns of eye movement.
- **Limited integration of spatial and temporal modeling** - most existing approaches use either CNN or LSTM independently, not in combination.
- **Small datasets** - many existing studies are validated on small datasets with fewer than 32 subjects, limiting generalizability.

### 5.2 Contribution of EyeMotionID

The proposed EyeMotionID framework addresses these gaps by:

1. **Introducing behavioral biometrics** - identifying users based on dynamic eye behavior (blink rhythm, gaze transitions, pupil movement) rather than static iris texture.
2. **Combining spatial and temporal deep learning** - integrating ResNet18 for spatial feature extraction with LSTM/GRU for temporal sequence modeling in a unified pipeline.
3. **Using a larger and more diverse dataset** - EyeDentify provides 51 subjects with 50 sessions each, significantly more than most existing studies.
4. **Comparative evaluation** - comparing static eye recognition with dynamic behavioral biometric authentication to quantify the improvement.
5. **Difficult to spoof** - since behavioral patterns are generated by neurological activity, they cannot be easily replicated even with physical access to eye images.
